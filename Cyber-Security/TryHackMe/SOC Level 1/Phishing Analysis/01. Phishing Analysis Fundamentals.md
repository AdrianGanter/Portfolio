## Phishing Analysis Fundamentals

### Overview

This room introduced the fundamentals of phishing email analysis from a SOC perspective, focusing on how to break down email structure, delivery mechanisms, and malicious indicators. The core objective was to build the ability to inspect raw email data (headers, body, and attachments) to identify phishing attempts and understand attacker intent.

---

### Email Fundamentals

An email is composed of a simple structure but contains multiple layers of investigative value:

- **Username**: Identifies the mailbox user
- **@ symbol**: Separates user and domain
- **Domain**: Identifies the mail server responsible for delivery

Understanding this structure is critical for identifying spoofed or lookalike domains commonly used in phishing campaigns.

---

### Email Delivery Process

Emails travel through multiple systems before reaching the recipient:

- **SMTP (Simple Mail Transfer Protocol)**: Used to send emails between clients and mail servers
- **DNS (Domain Name System)**: Resolves recipient mail server locations
- **POP3 / IMAP**: Used to retrieve emails

Key differences:

- **POP3**
  - Downloads emails to a single device
  - Emails may be removed from the server
- **IMAP**
  - Synchronises emails across multiple devices
  - Emails remain on the server

Understanding these protocols helps trace email origin and delivery paths during investigations.

---

### Email Structure for Analysis

A phishing investigation typically involves two key components:

#### Email Header
Contains metadata used for tracing origin and routing:

- From / To addresses
- Reply-To field
- Subject line
- Timestamp
- X-Originating-IP
- Authentication results

Headers are critical for identifying spoofing and infrastructure used by attackers.

#### Email Body
Contains the actual message content, often using:

- HTML formatting
- Embedded links
- Images
- Malicious attachments

Viewing the raw source of both headers and HTML content often reveals hidden indicators not visible in the rendered email.

---

### Attachment Analysis

Emails may contain embedded attachments that require deeper inspection.

Key indicators include:

- **Content-Type**: Defines file type (e.g., application/pdf)
- **Content-Disposition**: Indicates attachment and filename
- **Content-Transfer-Encoding**: Often base64 encoded payload

Attachments should always be decoded and analysed in a safe environment using tools such as CyberChef.

---

### Phishing Techniques

Phishing emails often share common traits designed to manipulate users:

- Spoofed sender addresses
- Urgency and fear-based messaging
- Brand impersonation (Microsoft, banks, etc.)
- Poor grammar or generic greetings
- Shortened or hidden links
- Malicious attachments disguised as legitimate files

Types of phishing include:

- **Spam / Malspam**: Bulk unsolicited emails
- **Phishing**: General credential harvesting attempts
- **Spear Phishing**: Targeted attacks on individuals or organisations
- **Whaling**: Targets executives (CEO/CFO level)
- **Smishing**: SMS-based phishing
- **Vishing**: Voice call-based phishing

These techniques are designed to bypass user suspicion and encourage immediate action.

---

### Safe Analysis Practices

During investigation, analysts must avoid interacting directly with malicious content.

Best practices include:

- Defanging URLs (e.g. `hxxp://example[.]com`)
- Inspecting raw email source instead of rendered views
- Using isolated environments for attachment analysis
- Avoiding direct clicks on links or attachments

---

### Business Email Compromise (BEC)

A key escalation of phishing attacks is BEC, where attackers compromise or impersonate legitimate internal email accounts to:

- Authorise fraudulent transactions
- Manipulate employees
- Bypass external phishing detection

BEC attacks are particularly dangerous due to their high level of trust exploitation.

---

### SOC Analyst Workflow Developed

This room reinforced a structured phishing investigation process:

1. Identify suspicious email indicators (sender, domain, subject)
2. Analyse email headers for origin and routing data
3. Inspect email body and HTML source for hidden content
4. Examine attachments and decode embedded payloads
5. Identify phishing techniques and social engineering patterns
6. Validate findings against known attack types (phishing, spear phishing, BEC)
7. Determine intent and severity of the email

---

### Key Takeaway

Phishing analysis is not just about spotting suspicious emails — it is about reconstructing the delivery chain, identifying deception techniques, and understanding attacker intent through technical artefacts and behavioural patterns.

This structured approach is essential for SOC triage, incident response, and preventing initial access attacks.
