# Cyber Kill Chain - My Understanding

## Overview

The Cyber Kill Chain is a cybersecurity framework developed by Lockheed Martin that breaks a cyber attack into seven distinct stages. It helps defenders understand how attackers operate, identify where an intrusion is occurring, and determine where security controls can be applied to disrupt the attack before the adversary achieves their objective.

As a SOC Analyst, understanding the Cyber Kill Chain helps me investigate alerts in context, recognize attacker behaviour, and identify opportunities to detect and stop threats throughout the attack lifecycle.

---

## The Seven Stages of the Cyber Kill Chain

### 1. Reconnaissance

The attacker gathers information about the target before launching an attack.

Examples:

* Open-Source Intelligence (OSINT)
* Email harvesting
* Social media research
* WHOIS lookups
* Network scanning

**Defender Focus:** Minimize exposed information, monitor scanning activity, and strengthen user awareness.

---

### 2. Weaponization

The attacker creates or acquires the tools required for the attack.

Examples:

* Malware development
* Malicious Office documents with macros
* Exploits targeting vulnerabilities
* Backdoors and remote access tools
* Phishing templates

**Defender Focus:** Threat intelligence, malware analysis, and detection rule development.

---

### 3. Delivery

The attacker delivers the malicious payload to the target.

Common methods:

* Phishing emails
* Malicious attachments
* Malicious links
* USB drops
* Watering hole attacks

**Defender Focus:** Email security, attachment scanning, web filtering, and security awareness training.

---

### 4. Exploitation

The attacker executes code or exploits a vulnerability to gain access.

Examples:

* Macro execution
* Zero-day exploits
* Exploitation of known CVEs
* Application vulnerabilities

Indicators:

* Unexpected process execution
* Suspicious command-line activity
* Registry modifications

**Defender Focus:** Vulnerability management, patching, EDR monitoring, and behavioural detections.

---

### 5. Installation

The attacker establishes persistence to maintain access.

Examples:

* Web shells
* Backdoors
* Registry Run Keys
* Startup folders
* Malicious Windows services
* Scheduled tasks

Techniques such as **timestomping** may also be used to hide evidence by modifying file timestamps.

**Defender Focus:** Persistence monitoring, endpoint detection, and file integrity monitoring.

---

### 6. Command and Control (C2)

The compromised system communicates with attacker-controlled infrastructure.

Common methods:

* HTTP/HTTPS beaconing
* DNS tunnelling
* Remote command execution
* Malware callbacks

The attacker can now remotely control the compromised device and issue commands.

**Defender Focus:** Network monitoring, DNS analysis, IDS/IPS alerts, and anomaly detection.

---

### 7. Actions on Objectives

The attacker achieves their intended goal.

Examples:

* Credential theft
* Privilege escalation
* Internal reconnaissance
* Lateral movement
* Data exfiltration
* Ransomware deployment
* Deleting backups and shadow copies

**Defender Focus:** Rapid detection, containment, incident response, and recovery.

---

## Why the Cyber Kill Chain Matters

The Cyber Kill Chain demonstrates that cyber attacks are not single events but a sequence of actions. Every stage provides defenders with an opportunity to detect, contain, or disrupt malicious activity before significant damage occurs.

As a SOC Analyst, I can use the framework to:

* Understand attacker behaviour
* Map security alerts to attack stages
* Improve threat hunting activities
* Develop more effective detections
* Identify gaps in defensive controls
* Support incident investigations and response efforts

---

## Limitations of the Cyber Kill Chain

While the Cyber Kill Chain remains a valuable framework, it is not a complete solution on its own.

The model was introduced in 2011 and primarily focuses on malware delivery and traditional network intrusions. Modern attackers frequently change file hashes, IP addresses, domains, and tools to evade detection, while also combining multiple tactics and techniques throughout an attack.

Another limitation is its reduced effectiveness against insider threats, where malicious activity originates from users who already have legitimate access to systems and data.

For this reason, security teams should use the Cyber Kill Chain alongside other frameworks such as:

* MITRE ATT&CK
* Unified Kill Chain
* Threat Intelligence frameworks

These provide broader visibility into modern adversary behaviour and detection opportunities.

---

## Key Takeaway

The Cyber Kill Chain provides a structured way to understand how attackers progress from initial reconnaissance through to achieving their objectives. By understanding each stage, defenders can identify opportunities to break the attack chain and prevent incidents from escalating.

My main takeaway from this room is that effective security is not just about detecting malware—it is about understanding attacker behaviour, recognizing where an adversary is within the attack lifecycle, and implementing controls that disrupt their progress before they reach their goal.
